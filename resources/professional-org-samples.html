Genre Sample: Professional Organization Statement

Professional Organization Statements

Three examples from different types of organizations. Notice how each balances advocacy with authority, and how tone shifts depending on audience and purpose.

National Association of Social Workers (NASW)

Statement on the Use of Artificial Intelligence in Social Work Practice

Adopted June 2025

The National Association of Social Workers recognizes that artificial intelligence tools are increasingly being integrated into social service delivery systems, including case management, risk assessment, and client intake processes. While these tools offer the potential to streamline administrative tasks and improve service coordination, they also raise significant ethical concerns that demand careful attention from practitioners, administrators, and policymakers.

NASW affirms the following principles:

  • AI tools must not replace the professional judgment of licensed social workers in decisions that affect client welfare, safety, or access to services.
  • Agencies implementing AI-driven systems must ensure transparency in how these tools make or inform decisions, particularly in child welfare, criminal justice, and mental health settings.
  • Social workers have a professional obligation to understand the limitations and potential biases embedded in AI systems they are asked to use.
  • Clients must be informed when AI tools are used in their care and must retain the right to request human review of any AI-assisted decision.

NASW calls on educational institutions to incorporate AI ethics and digital literacy into social work curricula, and urges federal and state agencies to develop regulatory frameworks that protect vulnerable populations from algorithmic harm.

Approved by the NASW Board of Directors, June 14, 2025
Disciplinary organization — advocacy + ethical guidance
American Federation of Teachers (AFT)

Resolution on Artificial Intelligence in Public Education

Adopted July 2024

WHEREAS, artificial intelligence technologies are being rapidly adopted across K–12 and higher education institutions, often without meaningful input from educators, students, or their families; and

WHEREAS, the implementation of AI grading systems, automated feedback tools, and predictive analytics in educational settings raises concerns about accuracy, bias, student privacy, and the erosion of the educator–student relationship; and

WHEREAS, educators are increasingly being asked to use AI tools without adequate training, support, or assurance that these tools have been independently evaluated for effectiveness or fairness;

THEREFORE BE IT RESOLVED, that the American Federation of Teachers:

  • Opposes the use of AI systems to replace the professional work of educators, including grading, feedback, and instructional decision-making;
  • Calls on school districts and institutions of higher education to include educators and their unions in all decisions regarding the adoption of AI tools;
  • Demands transparency and independent evaluation of any AI system used in student assessment or placement;
  • Advocates for federal legislation requiring that AI vendors disclose the data sources, methodologies, and known limitations of products marketed to educational institutions.
Adopted at the AFT National Convention, Houston, TX, July 2024
Labor organization / union — resolution format, formal "whereas" structure
American Nurses Association (ANA)

Position Statement: Nurses' Role in the Governance of AI-Assisted Clinical Decision Systems

Published March 2025

The American Nurses Association holds that registered nurses must be active participants in the design, implementation, and oversight of AI-assisted clinical decision support systems used in patient care settings. As the largest group of healthcare professionals in the United States, nurses bring essential clinical expertise and patient advocacy perspectives that must inform how these technologies are deployed.

ANA's position is grounded in the following:

  • AI-assisted tools that inform triage, diagnosis, or treatment planning must be treated as decision support, not decision replacement. The nurse retains clinical authority and professional accountability.
  • Healthcare organizations must establish interdisciplinary governance committees—including nursing representation—to evaluate AI tools before adoption and to monitor their performance on an ongoing basis.
  • Nurses must receive adequate education and training on the capabilities and limitations of AI systems integrated into their workflows.

ANA further recommends that nursing education programs at all levels incorporate content on health informatics, algorithmic bias, and the ethical implications of AI in clinical practice.

Approved by the ANA Board of Directors, March 8, 2025
