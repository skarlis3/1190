How to Annotate a PDF

Highlighting, Commenting, and Submitting Annotated Articles

For some assignments in this class, you'll need to annotate scholarly articles directly on the PDF — highlighting key passages, adding comments, and writing notes. This guide covers what to annotate, how to do it on whatever device you have, and how to make sure your annotations are saved when you submit.

What to Annotate (and Why)

Annotating isn't just highlighting everything that looks important. The goal is to have a conversation with the text — marking the parts that matter and writing down your thinking as you read. When you come back to the article later (for a paper, a discussion, or a project), your annotations become a record of what you noticed and what you were thinking.

Here are some things worth marking or commenting on as you read:

  • Main arguments or claims — What is the author's central point? What are they trying to convince you of?
  • Key findings or data — What did the study actually find? What numbers or results stand out?
  • Definitions or key terms — If the author defines a concept that's central to the article, mark it. You'll want to find it again.
  • Connections to other things you've read — Does this remind you of something from a news article, a social media post, or another scholarly source? Note the connection.
  • Things that confuse you — If a sentence or section doesn't make sense, mark it and write a quick note about what's unclear. You can come back to it, ask about it in class, or look it up.
  • Things that surprise you or that you disagree with — Your reactions matter. If something catches you off guard or you're skeptical, write that down.
  • Questions you want to explore further — Good research generates more questions. Capture them as they come up.

Your Annotations Don't Need to Be Formal

Write in your natural voice. "Wait, this contradicts what the NYT article said" or "I don't understand what this variable means" are perfectly good annotations. The point is to show that you're reading, thinking, and engaging — not to write polished paragraphs.

Annotating on a Computer (PC or Mac)

You have several free options for annotating PDFs on a computer. Pick whichever one you already have or feels most comfortable.

Google Drive as a Backup Option

If you upload a PDF to Google Drive, you can open it with Google Docs and add comments. However, this sometimes reformats the article and can make the layout messy. It works in a pinch, but the tools above will give you better results and keep your PDF looking clean.

Annotating on a Tablet (iPad or Android)

Tablets are actually great for annotating PDFs, especially if you have a stylus. You can highlight and write notes directly on the document like you would with a printed article and a pen.

Saving and Submitting Your Annotated PDF

The Most Important Step

Before you submit, open your saved PDF and check that your annotations are actually visible. Some tools save annotations in a way that only shows up in that specific app. Open the file in a different viewer (or email it to yourself and open it on another device) to make sure your highlights and comments survived the save. If they didn't, try using "Export" or "Save a Copy" rather than a regular save — this flattens the annotations into the file.

Checklist Before Submitting

  1. Open the PDF you're about to submit and scroll through it.
  2. Confirm that your highlights are visible on the pages.
  3. Confirm that your comments and notes appear (click or tap on highlights to check for attached notes).
  4. Confirm that your short annotation at the top of the first page is there (your 1–2 sentence note about how the article relates to your research).
  5. Upload the PDF to Canvas. Do not copy and paste text into the Canvas text box — submit the actual PDF file with annotations.

Naming Your Files

Name your files clearly so it's obvious what they are — for example, "Smith-2024-AI-Healthcare-Annotated.pdf" is much easier to work with than "download(3).pdf." This helps both you and your instructor.
