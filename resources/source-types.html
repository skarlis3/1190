Recognizing Source Types

Not all sources look the same, but there are patterns you can learn to spot. This page shows examples of common source types you may encounter when researching AI in your field. Each example is labeled and includes numbered callouts explaining what to look for.

Public Conversation

Social Media Post

Social media posts — on platforms like X (Twitter), Facebook, LinkedIn, Instagram, or Reddit — are public conversations, not vetted sources. Unless a post links directly to a news article or other published source, you should treat the post itself as a social media source, not a news source.

@nursingtoday_unofficial
AI is changing everything in nursing. Hospitals are already using AI triage tools and it's only going to get bigger. What do you think — is this good or bad for patients? 2
4:32 PM · Oct 14, 2024  ·  214 Reposts  ·  1,089 Likes 3
  • 1 The account name and handle are not affiliated with any official organization — anyone can create an account with a professional-sounding name.
  • 2 The post makes a claim but provides no source, data, or evidence. It also invites opinions rather than reporting facts.
  • 3 Engagement numbers (likes, reposts) tell you nothing about accuracy. Popular posts can still be wrong or misleading.
Public Conversation

Blog Post / Article

Blog posts are written by individuals or organizations and published without peer review or editorial oversight. They can be thoughtful and well-researched, but they can also be purely personal opinion. Look for the signals below to identify a blog.

nursinginsider.blogspot.com/2024/10/ai-in-nursing 1
Nursing Insider Blog 2
Why I Think AI Will Change Bedside Nursing Forever
Posted by Jamie L., RN · October 9, 2024 · 5 min read 3

I've been a nurse for twelve years, and nothing has surprised me more than watching AI tools roll out across our unit this past year. In my opinion, we're only at the beginning of a massive transformation...

Last month we started using an AI documentation tool that listens to patient interactions and auto-generates nursing notes. Honestly? I have mixed feelings. It saves me probably 45 minutes a shift, which sounds amazing — but I've caught errors in the generated notes twice already. Small ones, but still. That's not nothing when you're talking about a patient's chart.

I think nurses need to be in this conversation loudly and early, before hospital administrators make decisions about these tools without us. If you're a nurse and you haven't looked into what your hospital is piloting, now is the time. Leave a comment below if you want to share what you're seeing at your facility.

Tags: nursing, AI, opinion, technology, healthcare 4

  • 1 The URL includes "blog" or is hosted on a blogging platform (blogspot, Medium, Substack, WordPress, etc.).
  • 2 There is no named publication or editorial organization — just a blog name.
  • 3 The author is an individual, not a journalist or researcher writing for a publication. There may be no credentials listed at all.
  • 4 Tags or categories may include words like "opinion," "personal," or "lifestyle," signaling this is not a reported or researched piece.
Public Conversation

Opinion Article (Newspaper)

Opinion articles — also called op-eds or editorials — appear in newspapers and news sites alongside regular news coverage. They are written by journalists, experts, or guest contributors and express a personal point of view. They are labeled "Opinion" or "Editorial," but they can be easy to mistake for news articles if you're not looking carefully.

apnews.com/opinion/nursing-ai-future 1
AP News  |  OPINION 2
AI Will Never Replace the Human Heart of Nursing
By Dr. Patricia Okonkwo, Guest Contributor · November 2, 2024 3

The push to integrate artificial intelligence into nursing care is moving too fast, and I believe it will ultimately harm patients more than it helps them. No algorithm can replicate the intuition a seasoned nurse develops over years of patient care — and anyone who thinks otherwise has never spent a night on a hospital floor. 4

The technology may have its place, but until the evidence is clearer, hospitals should slow down and listen to frontline nurses. Instead, what we're seeing is the opposite: procurement decisions made by administrators chasing efficiency metrics, with bedside nurses consulted as an afterthought, if at all.

I've spent twenty years in critical care. I've watched patients survive because a nurse noticed something subtle — a change in skin color, a slight shift in breathing — that no monitoring system flagged. That is not inefficiency. That is nursing. We should be very careful before we let cost-cutting dressed up as innovation erode it.

  • 1 The URL may appear on a legitimate news site — this does not automatically make it a news article.
  • 2 Look for the word Opinion or Editorial near the publication name or headline. This is the clearest signal.
  • 3 Guest contributors are not staff journalists — they are outside writers invited to share a perspective.
  • 4 Opinion language — phrases like "I believe," "anyone who thinks otherwise," or strong absolute statements — signals this is a viewpoint, not a reported fact.
News

News Article

A news article reports on events, developments, or issues for a general audience. It is written by a journalist, edited by a publication, and aims to present facts rather than opinions. News articles cite sources like experts, studies, or official statements.

apnews.com/health/nursing-ai-hospitals-2024 1
AP News 2
Hospitals Expand AI Tools in Nursing Units, Raising Questions About Workload and Safety
By Marcus Webb, AP Health Writer · November 8, 2024 3

Hospitals across the country are deploying artificial intelligence tools to assist nursing staff with tasks ranging from patient monitoring to documentation, according to a new report from the American Hospital Association. 4

"We're seeing real efficiency gains, but nurses are also raising legitimate concerns about over-reliance on these systems," said Dr. Sandra Yuen, a health systems researcher at the University of Michigan. 5

The report found that 62 percent of large hospital systems have piloted at least one AI tool in nursing units since 2022, up from 31 percent two years earlier. The most common applications include predictive patient deterioration alerts, automated documentation, and AI-assisted scheduling. Smaller and rural hospitals have seen significantly slower adoption, largely due to cost barriers.

Nursing unions in several states have raised concerns about whether hospitals are using AI tools to justify reduced staffing ratios. The American Nurses Association has called for federal guidelines that would require hospitals to consult with nursing staff before deploying clinical AI systems. No such guidelines currently exist at the federal level, though three states have introduced legislation in the past year.

  • 1 The URL is on a recognized news outlet with no "opinion" or "blog" in the path.
  • 2 A named, established publication — not a personal blog or unknown site.
  • 3 The author is identified as a staff writer or reporter for the publication, not a guest or unnamed contributor.
  • 4 Claims are attributed to a named, external source — here, a published report from a known organization.
  • 5 Experts are quoted by name with their credentials and institutional affiliation clearly stated.
News

Professional Organization News

Professional organization news is published by organizations that represent people in a specific field. It is written for practitioners, not the general public, and tends to be more technical and field-specific than popular news. It may also reflect the organization's advocacy positions — see the Popular & Professional News reading for more on this.

ana.org/news/2024/ai-nursing-workforce-guidelines 1
American Nurses Association — Newsroom 2
ANA Releases New Guidelines on AI Integration in Nursing Practice
ANA Staff · October 28, 2024 3

The American Nurses Association today released updated practice guidelines addressing the integration of artificial intelligence tools in clinical nursing environments, citing concerns raised by member nurses about patient safety and professional accountability. 4

The guidelines recommend that AI tools be used only to support — not replace — clinical nursing judgment, and call for mandatory training before implementation. The full guidelines are available to ANA members in the Online Journal of Issues in Nursing. 5

The ANA also announced it will convene a national task force of frontline nurses, nurse educators, and informatics specialists to develop a framework for evaluating AI tools before hospital adoption. The task force is expected to release preliminary recommendations in the first quarter of 2025.

"Nurses must be at the table when these decisions are made," said ANA President Cveta Markovska, PhD, RN. "AI has real potential to reduce administrative burden and improve outcomes, but only if it's implemented thoughtfully, with the people who actually deliver care." Members can submit comments through the ANA member portal through December 31.

  • 1 The URL is on a professional organization's website (typically .org) with a news or newsroom section in the path.
  • 2 The publication is a named professional organization, not a general news outlet or personal blog.
  • 3 Articles may be written by organization staff rather than named individual journalists — this is normal for org newsrooms.
  • 4 The article reflects the organization's positions and concerns — this is advocacy-informed reporting, not neutral journalism.
  • 5 References to the organization's own publications or member resources are common — follow these links to find deeper, more detailed sources.
Public Conversation

Opinion Article (Academic Journal)

Academic journals sometimes publish opinion pieces, editorials, or commentary alongside peer-reviewed research. These can look very similar to scholarly articles — they appear in the same journals, use academic language, and are written by credentialed experts. The key difference is that they are not peer-reviewed research, and they typically have few or no references.

journals.nursingresearch.org/commentary/vol42/ai-ethics 1
Nursing Research Quarterly  |  COMMENTARY 2
Ethical Concerns About AI in Clinical Nursing: A Call for Caution
Dr. Renata Sousa, PhD, RN · Vol. 42, No. 4, 2024 3

The rapid deployment of AI in nursing practice raises profound ethical questions that the profession has not yet adequately addressed. I contend that we are moving recklessly, prioritizing efficiency over the fundamental moral obligations of nursing care. 4

The nursing community must slow down and engage in a broader ethical conversation before these tools become standard practice. Consider the implications of an AI system that flags a patient as low-risk moments before a rapid deterioration that an experienced nurse might have caught earlier. Who bears responsibility? The vendor? The hospital? The nurse who deferred to the system?

These are not hypothetical questions. They are the questions our professional organizations, licensing boards, and educators must be asking now — not after the technology is already embedded in every unit. The history of healthcare is full of innovations adopted with enthusiasm before their risks were fully understood. We owe our patients more careful stewardship than that.

References 5

  1. American Nurses Association. (2023). Code of Ethics for Nurses.
  2. Topol, E. (2019). Deep Medicine. Basic Books.
  • 1 The URL is on a legitimate academic journal site — this alone does not make it a scholarly research article.
  • 2 Look for labels like "Commentary," "Editorial," "Perspective," or "Letter" near the title or publication name. These signal opinion, not peer-reviewed research.
  • 3 The author may have impressive credentials (PhD, RN) — credentials alone don't make something a research article.
  • 4 First-person opinion language ("I contend," "I argue") signals this is a viewpoint piece, not a research report.
  • 5 The reference list is very short — only 2 items. A scholarly research article typically has many more references (often 20–50+). A short or missing reference list is a strong sign this is opinion, not research.
Scholarly / Academic

Scholarly Article

Scholarly articles report original research conducted by researchers and reviewed by other experts in the field before publication (called peer review). They follow a structured format, use technical language, and include an extensive list of references. They are the most heavily vetted type of source.

doi.org/10.1016/j.nursingresearch.2024.08.012 1
Journal of Nursing Research  |  ORIGINAL RESEARCH 2
Effects of AI-Assisted Triage on Patient Outcomes in Acute Care Nursing: A Randomized Controlled Study
Chen, M., Okafor, T., Patel, R., & Johansson, L. · Received March 2024 · Accepted July 2024 · Published August 2024 3

Abstract: This study examined the effects of AI-assisted triage software on patient wait times, triage accuracy, and nursing workload in three acute care facilities over a 12-month period. Using a randomized controlled design with 480 participants... 4

Introduction: The integration of artificial intelligence into acute care triage represents one of the most significant technological shifts in nursing practice in recent decades. While early studies have suggested potential improvements in patient flow and diagnostic accuracy, the effects on nursing workload and clinical decision-making remain poorly understood. This study addresses that gap through a rigorous controlled design across three facilities of varying size and patient population.

Methods: Participants were randomly assigned to either an AI-assisted triage condition or a standard triage condition. Primary outcomes included time to triage completion, triage accuracy as verified by physician review, and self-reported nurse workload using the NASA Task Load Index. Secondary outcomes included patient wait time, patient satisfaction scores, and incidence of triage errors. Data were collected at baseline, six months, and twelve months.

Sections: Introduction · Literature Review · Methods · Results · Discussion · Conclusion 5

Scholarly / Academic

White Paper / Report

White papers and reports are in-depth documents produced by organizations, government agencies, research institutions, or think tanks. They summarize research, analyze data, and often make policy or practice recommendations. They are not peer-reviewed in the same way as scholarly articles, but they are heavily researched and cited, and they carry significant weight in professional and policy conversations.

ahrq.gov/reports/nursing-ai-workforce-2024.pdf 1
Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) 2
Artificial Intelligence in the Nursing Workforce: Current Applications, Risks, and Recommendations for Practice
Prepared by the AHRQ Workforce Research Division · Report No. AHRQ-24-0047 · September 2024 3

This report synthesizes current evidence on the deployment of AI tools in nursing practice settings across U.S. healthcare institutions. Drawing on analysis of 34 peer-reviewed studies and survey data from 1,200 registered nurses, this report identifies key patterns in adoption, barriers to implementation, and risks to patient safety... 4

Key Findings: AI adoption in nursing settings has accelerated significantly since 2021, with documentation automation and predictive deterioration alerts representing the most widely implemented tools. Survey respondents reported moderate confidence in AI-assisted documentation (61%) but lower confidence in AI-generated clinical alerts (38%), citing concerns about false positives and alert fatigue. Nurses with fewer than five years of experience reported significantly higher rates of deference to AI recommendations than more experienced nurses.

Policy Recommendations: This report recommends that the Department of Health and Human Services develop minimum standards for pre-deployment testing of clinical AI tools in nursing settings; that hospitals be required to provide documented training before nurses are expected to use AI tools in clinical decision-making; and that nursing staff be formally included in procurement and evaluation processes for AI systems. Implementation of these recommendations would require coordination across federal agencies, state licensing boards, hospital accreditation bodies, and professional nursing organizations.

Sections: Executive Summary · Background · Methods · Findings · Policy Recommendations · Appendices 5
