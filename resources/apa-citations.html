Quick & Dirty Guide to APA Reference Citations

You don't need to build citations by hand — tools exist that pull the citation for you. This guide covers the best options depending on what kind of source you have. Always do a quick check after, but these methods get you most of the way there automatically.

For News Articles & Websites

ZoteroBib

ZoteroBib (bib.zotero.org) is a free, no-account-required citation generator made by the Zotero team — the same people behind one of the most respected academic citation managers. You paste in a URL and it pulls the metadata automatically. It handles news articles, websites, and journal articles.

  1. Go to bib.zotero.org
  2. Copy the URL of your article and paste it into the search bar, then press Enter
  3. ZoteroBib will generate the citation automatically
  4. Make sure APA is selected in the style dropdown at the top of the page
  5. Click Copy to Clipboard and paste it into your document

Your list stays saved in the browser tab as you work, so you can add multiple sources before copying them all at once.

Quick check — news articles & websites

ZoteroBib is accurate most of the time, but glance at these before you copy:

  • Date — confirm it matches the article's published date, not an "updated" date
  • Author — if it's blank, look for a byline on the article; no author is legitimate but don't assume
  • Publication name — should be the outlet (e.g., AP News), not the website domain
  • URL — confirm the link at the end goes to the right article

For Scholarly Articles

Method 1: Journal Article Page

Usually the cleanest and most reliable method. Most journal article pages have a built-in cite button that generates a complete citation including the DOI link.

Open the article's page on the journal's website — not just the PDF, but the actual article page. This is usually where you found the article, or you can get there by clicking the title link in Google Scholar or Semantic Scholar.

Journal of Nursing Research doi.org/10.1016/j.nursingresearch.2024.08.012
Original Research Article
Effects of AI-Assisted Triage on Patient Outcomes in Acute Care Nursing: A Randomized Controlled Study
M. Chen, T. Okafor, R. Patel, L. Johansson
Volume 42, Issue 4 August 2024 Pages 112–128
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nursingresearch.2024.08.012
  • 1 Look for a Cite button on the article page — it may also say Citation, Export Citation, or show a quotation mark icon. It is usually near the title, the abstract, or the article actions menu.

When you click the Cite button, a dialog or panel will appear. If you are given a choice of citation styles, select APA.

Export Citation ✕
APA 1 MLA Chicago BibTeX
APA Citation
Chen, M., Okafor, T., Patel, R., & Johansson, L. (2024). Effects of AI-assisted triage on patient outcomes in acute care nursing: A randomized controlled study. Journal of Nursing Research, 42(4), 112–128. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nursingresearch.2024.08.012 2
  • 1 Select APA from the style options. If APA is already selected or there is only one option, you are good to go.
  • 2 The citation should already include the full DOI link at the end — this is what makes the journal page method so convenient.

Copy the entire citation text and paste it into your Word document or wherever you are keeping your references.

Your finished citation should look like this:

Your final APA citation Chen, M., Okafor, T., Patel, R., & Johansson, L. (2024). Effects of AI-assisted triage on patient outcomes in acute care nursing: A randomized controlled study. Journal of Nursing Research, 42(4), 112–128. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nursingresearch.2024.08.012

What is a DOI?

A DOI (Digital Object Identifier) is a permanent link to a published article. It always starts with https://doi.org/ followed by a unique code. Including the DOI in your citation means anyone reading your paper can find the exact source you used, even if the article moves to a different URL. Always include it when it's available.

Method 2: Google Scholar

Use this method when you found your article through Google Scholar and don't have the journal page handy, or when the journal page doesn't have a Cite button. Google Scholar can generate an APA citation for almost any article in its index.

Search for the article title in Google Scholar. When you find the correct result, look for the Cite link below the result — it appears in the row of links beneath the snippet.

Scholar
Effects of AI-assisted triage on patient outcomes in acute care nursing
Effects of AI-assisted triage on patient outcomes in acute care nursing: A randomized controlled study
M Chen, T Okafor, R Patel, L Johansson — Journal of Nursing Research, 2024
This study examined the effects of AI-assisted triage software on patient wait times, triage accuracy, and nursing workload in three acute care facilities…
Cited by 14  ·  Related articles  ·  All 6 versions  ·  “ Cite 1 ”
[PDF] nursingresearch.org
  • 1 Click the Cite link — it appears in the small row of links beneath the article snippet, not on the right side. It looks like a quotation mark icon or the word "Cite."

A small dialog will appear with the citation already formatted in several styles. Copy the APA version — it is the first one listed.

Cite ✕
APA 1
Chen, M., Okafor, T., Patel, R., & Johansson, L. (2024). Effects of AI-assisted triage on patient outcomes in acute care nursing: A randomized controlled study. Journal of Nursing Research, 42(4), 112–128. 2
MLA
Chen, M., et al. "Effects of AI-assisted triage…" Journal of Nursing Research 42.4 (2024): 112–128.
  • 1 Copy the APA citation — it is the first style listed in the dialog.
  • 2 Notice that the Google Scholar citation does not include a DOI or link. You will need to add that in the next step.

Go back to the Google Scholar result and this time click the main article title — not the PDF link on the right. This will take you to the journal's article page.

  1. Click the article title (the blue link) to go to the journal page
  2. Look for a DOI on the page — it usually appears near the top, near the article title or publication info, and looks like: https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxxxxx
  3. Copy the full DOI link
  4. If there is no DOI listed, copy the URL from your browser's address bar instead
  5. Go back to your citation and paste the link at the very end, after a space

Your finished citation should look like this:

Google Scholar citation (before adding DOI) Chen, M., Okafor, T., Patel, R., & Johansson, L. (2024). Effects of AI-assisted triage on patient outcomes in acute care nursing: A randomized controlled study. Journal of Nursing Research, 42(4), 112–128.
Your final APA citation (after adding DOI) Chen, M., Okafor, T., Patel, R., & Johansson, L. (2024). Effects of AI-assisted triage on patient outcomes in acute care nursing: A randomized controlled study. Journal of Nursing Research, 42(4), 112–128. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nursingresearch.2024.08.012

A note on Google Scholar citations

Google Scholar's citations are auto-generated, which means they are occasionally imperfect — a period in the wrong place, an author's name formatted inconsistently, or a journal title not quite right. In practice these errors are rare, but it is worth a quick look before you submit.

The most common issue is simply the missing DOI, which is why Step 3 above is important. Beyond that, if something looks off, you can compare it against the journal page to double-check.

Quick check — scholarly articles

Whichever method you use, confirm these before submitting:

  • Author names — last name first, initials only (e.g., Chen, M.)
  • Year — should be the publication year, not an access date
  • Journal name — should be italicized and spelled out in full
  • DOI or URL — confirm it's there and that it works
