Finding Scholarly Sources

Scholarly research can be harder to find than news articles — especially for very current topics. This resource walks you through your best options, step by step.

Library Databases

Library databases are curated collections of academic journals, articles, and other publications that your library pays to give you access to. They are the traditional starting point for scholarly research because they are reliable, peer-reviewed, and well-organized.

You can access Macomb Community College's library databases through the Macomb Library website.

Important note about library databases at Macomb:

You may have limited success using the library databases for this assignment. Here's why:

  • As a community college, we have less database access than research-focused universities
  • Journal subscriptions are extremely expensive, and we can't pay for everything
  • Subscriptions are only getting more costly over time
  • Your topics are very recent — and the most current research is often behind paywalls even at large universities
  • You will need access to the most recent sources possible, which is where the tools below become more useful

Try the databases first, but don't be surprised if you need to use the methods below.

Google Scholar

Google Scholar (scholar.google.com) is a free search engine that indexes scholarly articles, theses, books, conference papers, and patents. It is one of the most useful tools available to community college students because it finds open-access versions of articles that would otherwise be behind paywalls.

Connecting your library account means Google Scholar will automatically show you a link to access articles through Macomb's subscriptions when available.

  1. Go to scholar.google.com
  2. Click the menu icon (☰) in the top left corner
  3. Click Settings
  4. Click Library links in the left menu
  5. In the search box, type Macomb Community College
  6. Check the box next to Macomb Community College when it appears
  7. Click Save

From now on, when a Macomb subscription is available for an article, you'll see a link to it on the right side of the search result.

Since your topic is recent, filtering by publication year is essential.

  1. Type your search terms into the Google Scholar search bar and press Enter
  2. On the results page, look at the left side panel
  3. You will see a Custom range option — click it
  4. Enter a start year (for AI topics, 2022 or later is recommended) and leave the end year blank
  5. Click Search to filter results

You can also use the quick year options like Since 2023 that appear in the left panel.

  • 1 Do not click the title. The title links to the journal's website, which is often behind a paywall. You will be asked to pay or log in.
  • 2 Check the journal name and year to confirm this is a real, recent publication in a relevant field.
  • 3 The snippet gives you a preview. Read it to decide if the article is relevant before clicking anything.
  • 4 Click the link on the right side — labeled [PDF], [HTML], or your library name. This is where you are most likely to get free access to the full article.
  • 5 "Related articles" and "Cited by" are useful for finding more sources — see the section below on using related articles.
  • 6 Results labeled [CITATION] have no free full text available. Google Scholar knows this article exists but cannot link to it. Skip these unless you can find it another way.

Once you find one good article, Google Scholar makes it easy to find more. Look at the links below any search result:

  • Related articles — shows articles on similar topics, identified by Google Scholar's algorithm. Great for broadening your search.
  • Cited by [number] — shows articles that have cited this one. Since they came after, they are often more recent and build on the same research. Great for finding newer work.

You can chain these — find one good article, click "Cited by," find another good article in those results, click "Related articles," and so on. This is called citation chaining and it is one of the most effective research strategies.

Semantic Scholar

Semantic Scholar (semanticscholar.org) is a free, AI-powered research tool developed by the Allen Institute for AI. It indexes millions of peer-reviewed papers and is particularly strong for recent research in health, technology, and science. It often surfaces open-access PDFs that are harder to find through Google Scholar.

  1. Go to semanticscholar.org
  2. Type your search terms into the search bar and press Enter
  3. Use specific terms — for example: artificial intelligence nursing triage or AI clinical decision making nursing

On the results page, use the filters on the left side to narrow down your results:

  1. Under Publication Date, set a range — for AI topics, start from 2022
  2. Under Publication Type, select Journal Articles to filter out conference papers and other types if needed
  3. Check Open Access if you want to limit to articles you can read for free immediately
  4. Under Fields of Study, you can select a relevant field like Medicine, Computer Science, or Nursing to narrow results

  • 3 The Open Access tag means there is a free full-text version available — look for the PDF link.

  • 5 Citations and Related Papers work the same way as in Google Scholar — use them to find more sources once you have found a good one.

When you click on an article in Semantic Scholar, the full article page has several useful features for expanding your research:

  • References tab — lists all the sources cited in this article. If an article is useful, its references are a goldmine of related sources.
  • Citations tab — shows all the articles that have cited this one. These are more recent and build on the same research.
  • Related Papers — Semantic Scholar's AI identifies papers on similar topics, even if they don't directly cite each other.
  • TLDR — Semantic Scholar provides an AI-generated one-sentence summary of the paper. This is useful for quickly deciding if an article is relevant, but always read the actual abstract before deciding to use it.

Using GenAI Tools to Help

GenAI tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are not databases — they cannot reliably search the internet for real articles on their own. However, they can be genuinely useful in two specific ways: helping you find a starting point for sources, and helping you figure out better search terms to use in Google Scholar or Semantic Scholar.

ChatGPT (with web search enabled) can sometimes surface real open-access articles with working links. The free version of ChatGPT has limited web search, and the free version of Claude does not reliably search for real academic sources, so results will vary. Treat anything GenAI gives you as a starting point to verify, not a confirmed source.

To get the best results, be very specific in your prompt. Here is a formula that works well:

Example prompt

Find me 3–5 real, peer-reviewed journal articles published after 2022 about [your topic]. I need open-access articles with working links to full PDFs. Do not make up sources — only list articles you can confirm exist with a real URL.

For example:

Example prompt

Find me 3–5 real, peer-reviewed journal articles published after 2022 about artificial intelligence in nursing practice. I need open-access articles with working links to full PDFs. Do not make up sources — only list articles you can confirm exist with a real URL.

Always verify every source GenAI gives you. GenAI tools can and do fabricate citations — real-sounding article titles, authors, and journals that do not actually exist. Before you use any source GenAI suggests:

  • Search for the article title in Google Scholar or Semantic Scholar
  • Confirm the article actually exists before using it
  • Confirm the link actually works and goes to the right article

This is one of the most reliable and useful ways to use GenAI for research. If your searches in Google Scholar or Semantic Scholar are not returning good results, ask a GenAI tool to help you brainstorm better search terms.

Example prompt

I am trying to find peer-reviewed research articles about AI in nursing. My searches aren't returning good results. Can you suggest 8–10 specific academic search terms or phrases I could try in Google Scholar? Include both broad and narrow terms.

GenAI is good at this because it understands academic vocabulary and can suggest terms you might not think of — like "machine learning clinical decision support" instead of just "AI nursing." Take the terms it gives you and plug them directly into Google Scholar or Semantic Scholar.

Another useful prompt

What are the academic or technical terms used in research literature for AI tools that help nurses monitor patients? I want to search for peer-reviewed articles and need the correct terminology.

Do not use GenAI to summarize your sources for you.

Using AI to summarize articles and then writing about those summaries means you are writing about what AI said, not what the research actually says. AI summaries can miss key details, misrepresent findings, or simply get things wrong. You need to read your sources yourself.
