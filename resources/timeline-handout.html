Project 2: Timeline Brainstorming

A reference guide for building your exhibit timeline. This is a brainstorming activity — nothing here is a commitment. Get your ideas out first, decide later.

Creative Reasoning

What you're doing with Project 2 is creative reasoning: imagination grounded in your research. You're not making stuff up from nothing — you're taking what you found in Project 1 and following the thread forward to see where it goes. Your research is the foundation; the creativity is in how you extend it.

This is sometimes called disciplined imagination (Weick, 1989) — the idea that creative thinking is most powerful when it's tethered to evidence and reasoning.

Brainstorm First, Decide Later

When building your timeline, don't hold back. Write down every idea, even the ones you're not sure about. Your timeline can branch — if you can think of three different things that could happen in 2032, write all three down. You'll pick the strongest path later. Right now, the goal is to get it all out.

Two Approaches

You can build your timeline in either direction — or both. Use whichever approach gets you unstuck, and you may find that you need to jump around a bit going backwards and forwards to get the connections to make sense.

Option A: Work Forward

Start with where things are right now (your real research from Project 1) and move forward in time. At each step, ask: what happens next? What changes because of this? Who responds, and how? Keep going until you reach 2050 (or whatever year you chose for those of you who wanted to go further into the future).

Option B: Work Backward

Start with your extreme future in 2050 — the utopian or dystopian version — and work backward. If this is the world in 2050, what had to happen in 2045 to get here? In 2040? In 2035? Build the chain of events in reverse until you connect back to the present.

For Working Forward

For each moment on your timeline, work through these steps. Remember: your timeline can branch (i.e. go in different directions). If more than one thing could happen, write them all down.

1. What happens next?

The plausible version. Based on what just happened on your timeline, what's the logical next step? What would you expect to see?

2. But what if…?

The escalated version. Take that plausible outcome and push it. What if it went further than expected? What if it worked too well, or failed worse than anyone thought? What if nobody stopped it?

3. Go big or go home!

Make it bigger, bolder, more extreme. While keeping this moment logically connected to what came before it, is there a way to take it even further? You're telling the over-the-top version of the story — your classified document is where the realistic version lives. So push it here.

Note: There may be points on your timeline where certain events need to be established before they escalate, so don't worry if you can't do this at every point on the timeline.

Questions to Help You Think

If you're stuck at any point on your timeline, try asking yourself some of these. They're designed to keep your thinking connected to people, not just technology.

  • Who is affected by this change? How do they feel about it?
  • Who could be taken advantage of in this situation? (dystopian)
  • Who could be helped by this? (utopian)
  • Who's making money from this?
  • What would the people in your field want to happen next?
  • What would they be afraid of happening next?
  • Who has the power to change this? Do they want to?
  • What rule, law, or policy would come out of this?
  • What job exists now that didn't before? What job disappeared?
  • What argument are people having about this?
  • What does a regular person's workday look like now?

Keep It Connected

Use "because" and "so" to check your path and that the progression of events feels logical. Each event on your timeline should connect to the one before it: This happened because of what came before, so this next thing happens. If you can't say "because" or "so" between two events, you might need an extra step or event in between.
