Project 2: Artifact Planning

Example Timeline → Projector View →

A guide for turning your timeline events into museum artifacts. For each moment on your timeline, you'll figure out what's happening, what kind of document or text would naturally exist there, and then plan out the details.

The Process

For each event on your timeline, work through these steps:

Step 1

What's happening at this moment?

Look at one event or node on your timeline. What's going on? What are the key things a museum visitor would need to understand about this point in time?

Step 2

What would exist here?

If this event really happened, what kinds of documents, posts, articles, or communications would people be creating? Think about who's involved and what they'd be writing, posting, or publishing. You might come up with several possibilities — write them all down, then pick the one that works best for your exhibit.

Step 3

Break it down

For the artifact you choose, plan out the details: What year is it from? What genre is it? Who's the audience? What's the purpose? What's the content or message? These details will guide you when you actually write or create the artifact.

Example: Walking Through It

Here's an example using one event from the example timeline about AI in higher ed.

Step 1: What's happening?

Degrees Become Meaningless

  • Employers start giving exams or more tasks for hiring
  • On-job training programs increase (but without multidisciplinary education)
Step 2: What would exist here?

If degrees are becoming meaningless and employers are switching to exams and training programs, what kinds of documents would exist in this world? Here are a few ideas:

LinkedIn Blog Post

A career coach or recruiter writing a post titled something like "How to Crush Your Interview Exams and Practical Demonstrations" — advice content that treats employer exams as the new normal.

Job Posting

A job listing that includes a degree as "nice to have" but under the training requirements section states that all new hires will be required to complete a company training program regardless of degree.

Not sure this one really gets the point across on its own — a job posting is kind of subtle. Might need a different genre, or maybe pair it with something else? Something to think about.
Reddit Post

Someone venting on a career subreddit about how many classes they have to take after starting a new job, and that if they don't pass the exams they get fired. Other commenters sharing similar experiences.

Step 3: Break it down

Pick one and plan the details. Here's the Reddit post:

Artifact Breakdown

Year:
2035
Genre:
Reddit post
Audience:
Other redditors who are looking for work or discussing employment and onboarding
Purpose:
To complain and maybe get advice about how to get through this
Content / Message:
Frustrated about the number of required classes and exams after starting a job; worried about getting fired if they fail; looking for solidarity or tips from others in the same situation

Why This Matters

Every choice you make here is a rhetorical choice. You're choosing a genre, an audience, a purpose, and a message — just like the writers and creators you studied in Project 1 did. The difference is that now you're the one making those decisions. The genre you pick shapes how visitors experience your exhibit: a Reddit rant hits differently than a corporate job posting, even if they're both about the same event on your timeline.
