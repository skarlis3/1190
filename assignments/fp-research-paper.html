For your final portfolio, you will revise your research paper one final time. This is your last opportunity to strengthen your paper using everything you've learned this semester.

Your final draft must still meet all of the original requirements from the Project 1: Research Paper assignment, including minimum length, source requirements, and APA formatting.

About Revision

Revision is all about taking a fresh set of eyes to previously created materials. Although this may sound like glorified proofreading, revision is a whole heap more than that, and to be done effectively, revision requires time, organization, and attention to detail. The danger with revision lies in the temptation to believe you already know all the ins and outs of your work, believing there is nothing left to "do" to it.

Editing is the final step as part of the revision, but revision is not editing. Revision is revisiting, rereading, rethinking, reworking, re-seeing.

Steps for Revision

  1. Start with your conference feedback. Read through the feedback from your individual conference. This is your starting point — not your entire revision plan, but the foundation for one.
  2. Create a revision plan. What are the major areas that need work? What are the minor things? Start with the major concerns (organization, argument, evidence, synthesis) before moving to minor ones (sentence-level clarity, grammar, formatting).
  3. Use your resources. Use the revision resources on this site, visit the Academic Success Center, or review the feedback you've received throughout the semester. You are not limited to just your conference feedback — use your own assessment of your paper, too.
  4. Revise your draft. This will likely not be a linear process — you'll find yourself going back over areas, making changes, revisiting your sources, then returning to the paper. Expect to put in substantial work and that there will be a lot of back and forth.
  5. Proofread. Once you are done with your revision, proofread your paper. You can use tools like the free version of Grammarly (works as a Chrome extension) or GenAI tools like Claude or ChatGPT to help with proofreading. If you use GenAI for proofreading, be cautious with the output to make sure it doesn't strip out your writing voice or change your ideas.

What I'm Looking For

  • Meaningful revision since the revised draft — not just proofreading, but substantive changes that strengthen the paper
  • Well organized with a clear structure
  • Claims are well supported by evidence from your sources
  • Sources are synthesized — integrated and in conversation with each other, not just dropped in one at a time
  • Readable with clear sentence flow and paragraph structure
  • Generally proofread and cleaned up
  • APA formatting — in-text citations and paper formatting follow APA conventions
