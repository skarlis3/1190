Project 2: Creating the Artifacts

Overview

Your exhibit will consist of a mix of "real" history (what is happening today) and "future" history (what you imagine happens between now and 2050).

Gathering Real Artifacts (Present Day)

You will gather artifacts from present day. These should come from your research on public conversations, news articles, and scholarly research. Include important snippets from these documents/items to demonstrate where it all started.

Requirements: Real Artifacts

  • Minimum of 3 existing artifacts.
  • Can be snippets of news articles, scholarly articles, or screenshots of online posts/conversations.
  • These must come from your Project 1 research.

Creating New Artifacts (Future)

This is the creative writing part — and the fun part. You will create fictional artifacts from the future (events that happen between now and 2050) that escalate the story toward your chosen extreme. Each artifact should push things a little further than the last.

Remember: your exhibit is telling the exaggerated version of the story. These artifacts should build toward your utopian or dystopian extreme. Start from something plausible and let it snowball.

Guidelines for Future Artifacts:

  • Escalation: Your artifacts should build on each other. Start with something that feels like a reasonable next step from your real research, then push it further and further as you move toward 2050.
  • Rooted in Research: Even though you are exaggerating, the starting point should be a logical continuation of the topics from Project 1, not entirely new topics.
  • Variety: Your real artifacts from Project 1 are probably social media posts, news articles or headlines, and snippets from scholarly research. Your future artifacts can include those same genres, but they can also branch into new ones. Think about what kind of document or text would naturally exist at each moment in your timeline. Some possibilities:
    • A job posting for a role that doesn't exist yet
    • A professional organization memo or policy announcement
    • An internal company email or HR notice about new AI protocols
    • A government regulation or executive order summary
    • A product listing or advertisement
    • A textbook excerpt or course catalog description from a future university
    • A patient/client intake form with new AI-related fields
    • A union statement or open letter
    • A breaking news headline or news article
    • A social media post, forum thread, or review
    The more ordinary and specific the document, the more convincing it tends to be. A mundane HR memo about "updated AI-supervision requirements" implies an entire world without having to explain it.
  • Visuals: At least one of these artifacts should be visual in nature, such as a chart, graphic, map, infographic, etc., to show how this change impacted the field.

In your exhibit, these artifacts will still be treated as "history" since our museum exhibits are taking place in 2050.

Requirements: Future Artifacts

  • Minimum of 5 future artifacts to show the escalation over time from today to 2050.
  • Example: You may want to include a social media post from 2030, a couple of artifacts from the late 2030s, and a few artifacts from the 2040s — each one a little more extreme than the last.
