Final Reflection

The Final Reflection is a reflection on your growth, strengths, and learning this semester. You'll curate artifacts from your work — excerpts, screenshots, before/after comparisons — and reflect on what they show about you as a writer and student. This is separate from your final revised draft, which is submitted on its own.

There are no design requirements — focus on the content. Use whatever layout, fonts, or colors you want as long as it's readable.

Format Options

Choose one:

  1. PowerPoint or Google Slides — Build your reflection as a slide deck.
  2. Google Site (or another website) — Build your reflection as a webpage. If you use something other than Google Sites, you're responsible for your own tech — just make sure I can access it via a link and that you can embed documents or screenshots. Your site must stay live through the end of the semester.

See Submitting Your Portfolio for format-specific submission instructions for both options.

Want to use a different format for your reflection? Check with me first to get it approved.

Where to Find Artifacts

Your artifacts can come from anywhere in the work you've done this semester. Here are some ideas:

  • Weekly writings
  • Snippets from any of your research paper drafts (rough, revised, or final)
  • A tech-free writing freewrite
  • A museum artifact you wrote for Project 2
  • Highlights, notes, or annotations from your research
  • Screenshots of your research process (database searches, organizing sources, etc.)
  • Prewriting or any other work you did as part of preparing for writing in ENGL 1190
What Makes a Thoughtful and Well-Developed Reflection?

A strong reflection goes beyond summarizing what you did. It shows that you're thinking critically about your own work and growth. Here's the difference:

  • Surface-level: "I revised my paper and made it better."
  • Thoughtful: "I noticed my argument was stronger when I reorganized by theme instead of by source, because it let me show how different perspectives connect to each other instead of just listing what each source said."

A well-developed reflection also means:

  • Your artifacts are well chosen and actually demonstrate the point you're making — don't just grab something random; pick something that clearly shows what you're reflecting on
  • All six sections are present and substantive
  • You're specific — use examples from your own writing to illustrate your points rather than speaking in generalities

What to Include

Your Final Reflection should cover all six of the following. Reflections can be in paragraph form or bullet points, as long as there's enough depth to show you're thinking critically about your own work — not just summarizing what the artifact is.

  1. Growth as a Writer (Before & After) — Artifact required
  2. A Strength (Artifact + Reflection) — Artifact required
  3. Revision (Before & After) — Artifact required
  4. Growth as a Student (Flexible) — Artifact required
  5. Advice for Next Semester — Written response only
  6. Final Thoughts — Written response only

See the Full Prompts Below

The list above is just an overview. See the Reflection Prompts section below for full descriptions of what to include and what to reflect on for each one.

Reflection Prompts

1. Growth as a Writer (Before & After)

Artifact required: Yes

Show something from earlier in the semester that represents a starting point in your writing — this could be from a weekly writing, your rough draft, or any earlier assignment. Then show something from later in the semester that demonstrates growth in comparison.

Reflect on: What changed? What do you notice about the difference between the two? What do you think contributed to that growth?

2. A Strength (Artifact + Reflection)

Artifact required: Yes

Show an artifact that demonstrates something you think you do well as a writer. This could be a high-order concern like organization, building a logical argument, or using evidence effectively — or a middle-order concern like flow, transitions, or voice.

Reflect on: What is the strength you're highlighting? How do you know this is something you do well? When did you start to notice it or develop confidence in it?

3. Revision (Before & After)

Artifact required: Yes

Show a specific passage, section, or element of your writing before and after revision. This could come from your rough draft vs. revised draft, or from any point in the semester where you revised something and it got better.

Reflect on: What did you change and why? What made this revision work? What did you learn about revision from doing this?

4. Growth as a Student (Flexible)

Artifact required: Yes

This one is more open. Show an artifact that demonstrates how you grew as a student this semester — not just as a writer, but in how you approach learning, research, or college in general. You might need to get a little creative with this one, and that's okay.

Some examples of what this could look like:

  • A comparison of your early source list (from a weekly writing) vs. your final references — showing how your research skills developed
  • An annotated source showing how you learned to read and engage with scholarly articles
  • A screenshot of your planning/organization process
  • Evidence of learning to use a tool or resource (library databases, APA formatting, etc.)
  • Something that shows a shift in how you approach deadlines, feedback, class participation, or asking for help

These are just examples — if you have a different way to show your growth as a student, go for it.

Reflect on: What changed about how you approach school, learning, or this class specifically? Why does this artifact represent that change?

5. Advice for Next Semester

Artifact required: No

Just answer the question:

What advice would you give to students taking this class next semester?

6. Final Thoughts

Artifact required: No

Choose one (or more) of the following prompts:

  • What is the one thing you want to keep working on with your writing or research skills?
  • What do you think the most helpful thing you learned this semester is?
  • What do you want to remember the next time you write a research paper?
  • Any final thoughts on the class and your work in general?

Grading

The Final Reflection is graded as part of your final portfolio, not on its own. Both the Final Reflection and the Research Paper (Final Draft) are considered together as one holistic grade. See the Final Portfolio Grading page for the full grading criteria.
