Overview

Your job today is to read your classmates' papers as a reader — not as an editor. You're helping the author see their paper from the outside: what's coming through clearly, what's confusing, where the support feels strong, and where it feels thin.

For each paper you review, you'll complete two parts:

  • Part A Paper Map — Read through the paper and build a quick outline of what you see. Post this in the comments section in Canvas.
  • Part B Feedback on the Draft — Leave comments directly on the paper in Canvas using the annotation tools, pointing to specific spots that are working well or could use improvement.

You may also discuss papers verbally, but make sure all feedback is left in Canvas so the author can refer back to it later.

Part A Paper Map

Start by reading the paper all the way through and building a Paper Map. This is a quick reverse outline that helps you get the lay of the land before you start leaving detailed feedback. It also gives the author a useful picture of how their paper reads to someone seeing it for the first time.

Post your Paper Map in the comments section in Canvas (not as annotations on the paper). Answer the following:

Paper Map Questions

  1. What is the topic and field?
    In a sentence, what is this paper about?
  2. What does the introduction set up?
    Based on the intro, what main argument or direction does it seem like the paper is heading? If you can't tell, note that — that's useful feedback for the author.
  3. What are the major sections or sub-points?
    List the main chunks of the paper and what each one is about. These might have section headings, or they might not — just describe what you see.
    If you're having trouble identifying distinct sections or sub-points, that's worth noting to the author. It likely means the paper's organization could be clearer.
  4. What sources show up in each section?
    For each section, do a quick tally of what types of evidence you see:
    • Social media / public conversations: ___
    • News articles: ___
    • Scholarly sources: ___
    This doesn't need to be exact — a quick scan is fine.
  5. Overall, does the support feel sufficient?
    Are there sections that feel thin or unsupported? Are any source types missing entirely? Does the evidence feel balanced across the paper, or does it cluster in some sections and disappear in others?

What to Look For

  • Does the introduction give enough background for someone unfamiliar with the field to follow the rest of the paper?
  • Are sources in conversation with each other, or are they just listed one after another without connection?
  • Are there places where evidence is dropped in without any discussion of what it means? (See the TED Paragraphs resource for what this should look like.)
  • Does the paper flow logically from one section to the next? Are there transitions, or does it feel like jumping between unrelated ideas?
  • Are there spots where you got confused or had to re-read to understand what was being said?

Feedback Requirements

  • Leave feedback on the paper using Canvas (see instructions below).
  • Leave at least one comment that:
    • addresses a high- or middle-order concern that could use improvement (organization, details, paragraph structure, examples, flow, transitions, etc.).
    • points to a specific spot in the paper that illustrates the issue.
  • Leave at least one comment about something that's working well (ideally connected to a high-/middle-order concern).
  • Do not leave later-order concerns (grammar/spelling/mechanics) unless they block readability. If so, make one note to explain the issue.
  • Do not edit your classmate's draft (no direct grammar fixing in the text).

Tips for Giving Good Feedback

Keep in Mind

  • You don't have to prescribe exact fixes. Focus on describing what you notice as a reader. "This section felt like it jumped topics" is more helpful than trying to rewrite it for them.
  • Use feedback sandwiches when possible. Pair suggestions for improvement with something that's working well nearby.
  • Be specific. "The third paragraph is confusing" is okay. "The third paragraph starts talking about job loss but then shifts to ethics without a transition" is much better.
  • It's a rough draft. Expect it to be rough. Your job is to help the author figure out where to focus their revision energy, not to find everything that needs fixing.

Instructions for Using Canvas for Peer Review

  1. Go to Modules and open the assignment titled Rough Draft with Peer Review.
  2. Click one of your assigned peer reviews.
  3. Click View Feedback.
  4. Use the double-sided arrows to expand the document to full screen.
  5. Use the annotation tools (highlighter, comment tool) to leave feedback on the draft.

Viewing Peer Review Feedback

  • Open the assignment in Modules → Rough Draft with Peer Review.
  • Your peer's summary comment appears in the comments panel on the right.
  • To view annotations on the paper, click View Feedback. Comments should appear alongside your draft.
