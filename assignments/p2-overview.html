Project 2: Museum Exhibit from the Year 2050

Attendance is Mandatory

You must be in class on Exhibit Day to display your project. Late projects are not accepted except in extenuating circumstances.

Overview

For Project 2, you are going to take the research and conclusions you drew from Project 1 to create a multimodal "museum exhibit" display. You will include artifacts from your Project 1 and new fictional artifacts from the near future, up until 2050, to tell the story of how AI changed your field — pushed to an intentionally exaggerated extreme.

The Premise

The year is 2050. You are curating an exhibit on how AI changed your field. But here's the twist: your exhibit tells the over-the-top version of the story. The best-case scenario pushed to its most utopian extreme, or the worst-case scenario pushed to its most dystopian extreme. Think big, go wild, have fun with it.

Then, tucked inside your exhibit, there's a classified document — a reality check. Based on your actual research from Project 1, what was probably going to happen? ("But here's what really happened...")

  • Take research/conclusions from Project 1.
  • Create a museum exhibit for the year 2050.
  • Include artifacts from Project 1 (the past) and new fictional artifacts from the near future (2027–2050).
  • Push the story to an exaggerated extreme — utopian or dystopian.
  • Include a classified document that offers the more realistic, nuanced version.

What Is an Artifact?

An artifact is an object made by a human.

The long version: "An object made or modified by human workmanship, as opposed to one formed by natural processes" (Oxford University Press, n.d.).

Why This Project?

All semester, you've been researching how AI is being discussed in your field — reading social media posts, news articles, and scholarly research. Along the way, you've been analyzing multimodal texts without necessarily thinking of them that way: the clickbait headline designed to get a reaction, the TikTok video that uses text overlays and editing to build an argument in 60 seconds, the scholarly article that uses charts and data visualizations to support its claims. Now you're moving from analyzing those genres to creating them — making the same kinds of rhetorical choices that the writers and creators you've been studying made.

This project is also about understanding how framing shapes meaning. Your research from Project 1 doesn't change between the exhibit and the classified document — but the story you tell with it does. The same evidence can build toward a utopian future or a dystopian collapse depending on what you emphasize, what you leave out, and how you sequence it. Recognizing how that works is one of the most important things you can take from a research writing class, because it's happening in every news cycle, every social media feed, and every conversation about AI.

The classified document is where you put those two things together: stepping back from the narrative you built, evaluating it against your actual research, and articulating where the exaggerated version departs from what the evidence supports.
