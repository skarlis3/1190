Project 2: Building the Exhibit

Museum Exhibit Display

The final step will be putting together your museum display. For the exhibit day, we will be meeting in a large classroom, so you will have more space.

Display Options

  1. A classroom table (a desk/table that fits two students).
  2. An area of floor space to put your display up (for larger/taller displays).
  3. A combination of the two.

Important Note

Make sure you are able to set up your exhibit in 5–10 minutes! If you think you need more time than that, come talk to me to see if we can get you in the room a little early.

Exhibit Requirements

  • Theme: Has a clear theme (i.e., Utopian or Dystopian extreme).
  • Escalation: The exhibit should build from real artifacts (present day) toward your exaggerated future. Visitors should be able to feel the story getting more extreme as they move through the display.
  • Design: Designed and implemented to represent both your field and your theme (i.e., decorate/design your exhibit with purpose).
  • Cohesion: Museum artifacts displayed cohesively.
  • Connections: Clear connections and additional information (such as on museum placards or visual design elements) to help guide the reader through the display.
  • Classified Document: Your exhibit must include a sealed or folded classified document. See below for details.
  • Optional: You may also include memes, images, etc., to round out your museum exhibit. Have some fun with this!

Placards

The Classified Document

Your exhibit tells the exaggerated version of the story. The Classified Document is where you get real.

This is a physical document — sealed in an envelope, folded and marked "CLASSIFIED," or otherwise presented as something a museum visitor would need to open or unfold. It should feel like discovering the truth behind the exhibit's sensationalized narrative.

What's Inside

Your classified document has three parts:

  1. The Reality Check — Based on your Project 1 research, what was the more realistic trajectory for AI in your field? This is your "But here's what really happened..." moment. What does the actual evidence from your sources suggest is likely? Where does the exaggerated version of your exhibit depart from what's probable? This section should reference specific findings from your research.
  2. References — A list of the sources used across your entire exhibit (both the display and this document). Use APA format.
  3. Reflection — A short reflection on the project: What did you learn from building this exhibit? What was it like to push your research to an extreme and then pull it back to reality?

Requirements

  • The reality check section should be at least 300 words and should reference your Project 1 research to support your reasoning.
  • References should be in APA format and cover all sources used in the exhibit and this document.
  • The reflection should be at least 150 words.
  • The document should be physically part of your exhibit — in a sealed envelope, folded with "CLASSIFIED" on the outside, or presented in a way that invites visitors to open it.
