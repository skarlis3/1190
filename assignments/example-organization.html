Project 1: Example Organization

Sample Ways to Structure Your Research Paper

At a Glance

  • There is no single "correct" way to organize Project 1.
  • Each model below is a full paper structure made of sections (not single paragraphs).
  • Choose the model that best fits your topic, field, and main idea.
  • You can mix and match ideas from different models as long as your paper stays clear and organized.

Overview

Project 1 asks you to put different kinds of sources into conversation in order to explore how AI is being used, discussed, and debated in your chosen field. The models below give you four possible ways to organize your paper.

For each model, you will see:

  • a short description,
  • when the model might work well for you,
  • a generic outline, and
  • a sample outline using technical theatre as the example field.

You can use one of these models, adapt one, or combine ideas from more than one. Whatever you choose, your paper must still meet all requirements in the Project 1: Research Paper Overview.

Possible Organization Models Summary

Model 1: Organized by Sub-Topic

This model organizes your paper around 2–3 major sub-topics or themes that came up in your research. Each section mixes together public conversations, news, and scholarly sources.

Model 2: Organized by Stakeholders

This model organizes your paper by who is affected by AI in your field. Each section focuses on a different stakeholder group and mixes together public conversations, news, and scholarly sources.

Model 3: Organized by Research Type

This model organizes your paper by type of source: public conversations, news coverage, and scholarly research. You then add a separate section where you put these groups in conversation with each other.

Model 4: Hybrid — Public Conversation First, Then Sub-Topics

This model starts with a section on public conversations and professional perspectives to establish what people in the field are currently saying and feeling about AI. Then it moves into 2–3 sub-topics that mix all source types together.

Model 1: Organized by Sub-Topics

This model organizes your paper around 2–3 major sub-topics or themes that came up in your research. Each section mixes together public conversations, news, and scholarly sources.

When this model might work well for you

  • Your research keeps circling around a few big issues or themes.
  • You like the idea of putting all source types together inside each section.
  • You want to focus on patterns and connections across your sources.

Generic Outline (Organized by Sub-Topics)

  1. Introduction
    Introduce AI in your field and the main sub-topics you will focus on.
  2. Background
    Brief context so a reader outside the field can follow your paper.
  3. Section on Sub-Topic 1 (multiple paragraphs)
    Mix public conversations, news, and scholarly sources related to the first major theme.
  4. Section on Sub-Topic 2 (multiple paragraphs)
    Mix all source types again around the second major theme.
  5. Section on Sub-Topic 3 (multiple paragraphs, optional)
    A third theme, if your research supports it.
  6. Synthesis Section (multiple paragraphs)
    Connect the sub-topics and show how they relate, overlap, or conflict.
  7. Conclusion
    Reflect on what these themes together show about AI in your field.

Example Outline (Technical Theatre — by Sub-Topics)

  1. Introduction
    Briefly introduce technical theatre and how AI tools are starting to appear backstage.
  2. Background
    Short overview of technical theatre roles so the reader can understand the later sections.
  3. Section on Sub-Topic 1: Designers & AI Art (multiple paragraphs)
    • AI art for early brainstorming
      Use public posts + news to show how designers use AI for quick concept sketches.
    • AI art for actual design choices
      Use public posts + scholarly sources to show concerns about creativity, ownership, and artistic identity.
  4. Section on Sub-Topic 2: AI-Assisted Lighting & Sound Cues (multiple paragraphs)
    • Automation in cue programming
      Use public posts + scholarly sources to show how automation affects training and hands-on skills.
    • Reliability during performances
      Use public posts + news to show worries about cues misfiring or needing human oversight.
  5. Section on Sub-Topic 3: Safety & Risk (multiple paragraphs)
    • Safety issues with automated systems
      Use news + scholarly sources to show safety concerns and the limits of relying on AI.
    • Balancing speed and safe practice
      Use public posts + news + scholarly sources to show tension between "saving time" and maintaining safe rehearsal processes.
  6. Synthesis Section (multiple paragraphs)
    • How design and technical concerns connect
      Show how worries about creativity relate to worries about training and skills.
    • Shared safety concerns
      Highlight safety issues appearing across design, cueing, and automation topics.
    • What remains unclear
      Point out ongoing debates and questions the field has not fully answered yet.
  7. Conclusion
    Summarize the complexity of AI in technical theatre and note any open questions.
Model 2: Organized by Stakeholders

This model organizes your paper by who is affected by AI in your field. Each section focuses on a different stakeholder group and mixes together public conversations, news, and scholarly sources.

When this model might work well for you

  • Your field clearly involves different groups (for example: workers, managers, clients, students, patients, etc.).
  • Your sources talk a lot about how AI affects these groups differently.
  • You want to highlight conflicts or agreements between groups.

Generic Outline (Organized by Stakeholders)

  1. Introduction
    Introduce AI in your field and the main stakeholder groups you will focus on.
  2. Background
    Brief explanation of what different groups in the field do.
  3. Section on Stakeholder Group 1 (multiple paragraphs)
    How Group 1 experiences or responds to AI, using public, news, and scholarly sources.
  4. Section on Stakeholder Group 2 (multiple paragraphs)
    How Group 2 experiences or responds to AI, again using multiple source types.
  5. Section on Stakeholder Group 3 (multiple paragraphs)
    A third group's experiences or concerns, if your research supports it.
  6. Section on Stakeholder Group 4 (multiple paragraphs, optional)
    A fourth group, if relevant to your field and sources.
  7. Synthesis Section (multiple paragraphs)
    Compare and contrast stakeholder perspectives. Show shared concerns and differences in priorities.
  8. Conclusion
    Reflect on what this range of viewpoints reveals about AI in your field.

Example Outline (Technical Theatre — by Stakeholders)

  1. Introduction
    Briefly introduce AI entering technical theatre and explain that different groups in the field experience these changes in different ways.
  2. Background
    Short overview of the main stakeholder groups: designers, technicians, theatre institutions, and audiences.
  3. Section on Stakeholder Group 1: Designers (multiple paragraphs)
    • Artistic concerns with AI-generated design
      Use public posts + scholarly sources to show worries about creativity, authorship, and artistic identity.
    • Early experimentation with AI tools
      Use news + public posts to show designers testing AI for projection mapping or concept art.
  4. Section on Stakeholder Group 2: Technicians & Operators (multiple paragraphs)
    • Impact of automation on training
      Use public posts + scholarly sources to show concerns about losing chances to learn hands-on cue programming.
    • Reliability and safety during shows
      Use public posts + news to highlight fears about automated cues misfiring or needing backup systems.
  5. Section on Stakeholder Group 3: Theatre Institutions / Companies (multiple paragraphs)
    • Efficiency and cost-saving motivations
      Use news + public responses to show how institutions justify adopting AI tools to reduce time and labor costs.
    • Tension between institutional goals and worker priorities
      Use scholarly sources + public posts to demonstrate conflicts between efficiency and safety/artistic values.
  6. Section on Stakeholder Group 4: Audiences (multiple paragraphs, optional)
    • Curiosity and skepticism about AI in live theatre
      Use public comments + news/op-eds to show mixed audience reactions to AI-driven effects.
    • Expectations about authenticity
      Use scholarly sources + news to show debates about whether AI changes the feeling of "live" theatre.
  7. Synthesis Section (multiple paragraphs)
    • Shared concerns
      Show how designers, technicians, and audiences all express concerns about quality, authenticity, or safety.
    • Differences in priorities
      Compare institutions' emphasis on efficiency to workers' emphasis on training and reliability.
    • What remains unsettled
      Note ongoing debates about how AI should be used in future productions.
  8. Conclusion
    Summarize how AI impacts stakeholders differently and highlight ongoing questions the field must navigate.
Model 3: Organized by Research Type

This model organizes your paper by type of source: public conversations, news coverage, and scholarly research. You then add a separate section where you put these groups in conversation with each other.

When this model might work well for you

  • You want a very clear, straightforward structure.
  • You notice strong differences between what everyday people say, what the news reports, and what scholars argue.
  • You feel confident writing a separate section that compares and connects these perspectives.

Generic Outline (Organized by Research Type)

  1. Introduction
    Briefly introduce AI in your field and your overall angle or main idea.
  2. Background
    Short explanation of the field so a reader can follow your paper.
  3. Section on Public Conversations (multiple paragraphs)
    How everyday people in the field talk about AI in social media or community spaces.
  4. Section on News Coverage (multiple paragraphs)
    How journalists and industry news describe AI's role, changes, or controversies in the field.
  5. Section on Scholarly Research (multiple paragraphs)
    What researchers and experts say about AI in this field (findings, concerns, predictions).
  6. Synthesis Section (multiple paragraphs)
    Put public, news, and scholarly perspectives in conversation. Show where they overlap, conflict, or raise new questions.
  7. Conclusion
    Reflect on what this range of sources reveals about AI in the field and what questions remain.

Example Outline (Technical Theatre — by Research Type)

  1. Introduction
    Briefly introduce AI's emerging role in technical theatre and explain that you will compare how different kinds of sources talk about these changes.
  2. Background
    Short overview of technical theatre so the reader can understand the rest of the paper.
  3. Section on Public Conversations (multiple paragraphs)
    • Designer debates about AI art
      Use public posts to show disagreements about AI-generated concept art and concerns about creativity.
    • Technicians discussing automated cues
      Use public posts/comments to show worries about skill loss, reliability, or safety in AI-assisted cueing.
  4. Section on News Coverage (multiple paragraphs)
    • Reports on AI-assisted lighting and projections
      Use news articles to show how professional venues are adopting AI tools for lighting or projections.
    • Articles discussing automation and job concerns
      Use news sources to highlight conversations about job shifts, training, and efficiency pressures.
  5. Section on Scholarly Research (multiple paragraphs)
    • AI and creative authorship in the arts
      Use scholarly research to explore ethical and artistic questions about AI-generated design work.
    • Safety and reliability in automated technical systems
      Use scholarly research to discuss risk management and the need for human oversight.
  6. Synthesis Section (multiple paragraphs)
    • Comparing everyday concerns to media framing
      Show how public posts focus on personal worries (training, creativity) while news focuses more on industry trends.
    • Comparing news stories to scholarly analysis
      Show where scholarly research supports, complicates, or challenges the issues raised in news coverage.
    • What all sources reveal together
      Highlight tensions and uncertainties about AI's impact on technical theatre.
  7. Conclusion
    Reinforce how looking at public, news, and scholarly sources together gives a fuller picture of AI in technical theatre.
Model 4: Hybrid — Public Conversation First, Then Sub-Topics

This model starts with a section on public conversations and professional perspectives — what people in the field are currently saying, feeling, and debating about AI. This opening section establishes the current mood and real-world context before you move into deeper analysis. After that, the paper shifts into 2–3 sub-topics that mix all source types together.

When this model might work well for you

  • You want to ground your paper in what's actually happening right now — the conversations, concerns, and excitement people in the field are expressing.
  • Your public conversation sources and professional organization perspectives are strong and set up the rest of the paper well.
  • You want to establish the current feelings and attitudes about AI in your field before diving into specific sub-topics.

Generic Outline (Hybrid — Public Conversation First, Then Sub-Topics)

  1. Introduction
    Introduce AI in your field and your overall angle or main idea.
  2. Background
    Brief context so a reader outside the field can follow your paper.
  3. Section on Public Conversations & Professional Perspectives (multiple paragraphs)
    What people in the field are currently saying about AI — in social media, community spaces, blog posts, and professional organization publications. This section establishes the current conversation before you get into specific issues.
    Source types for this section might include: Reddit/LinkedIn/forum posts, blog articles from people in the field, position statements or articles from professional organizations (from their websites), and other public-facing sources.
  4. Section on Sub-Topic 1 (multiple paragraphs)
    Mix public conversations, news, and scholarly sources related to the first major theme.
  5. Section on Sub-Topic 2 (multiple paragraphs)
    Mix all source types again around the second major theme.
  6. Section on Sub-Topic 3 (multiple paragraphs, optional)
    A third theme, if your research supports it.
  7. Conclusion
    Reflect on what these perspectives and themes together reveal about AI in your field.

Example Outline (Technical Theatre — Hybrid)

  1. Introduction
    Briefly introduce AI's emerging role in technical theatre and explain that the paper will first look at what people in the field are saying before examining specific issues.
  2. Background
    Short overview of technical theatre so the reader can understand the rest of the paper.
  3. Section on Public Conversations & Professional Perspectives (multiple paragraphs)
    • What technicians and designers are saying online
      Use Reddit posts, forum discussions, and social media to show the range of reactions — excitement, concern, skepticism — from people working in the field.
    • What professional organizations are saying
      Use a blog article from a theatre technology publication and a position statement from a professional organization like USITT (United States Institute for Theatre Technology) to show how the field's institutions are framing the conversation about AI.
  4. Section on Sub-Topic 1: AI and Creative Design (multiple paragraphs)
    • AI-generated concept art and set design
      Use public posts + news to show how designers are experimenting with AI for early brainstorming.
    • Concerns about creativity and authorship
      Use scholarly sources + public posts to explore debates about whether AI-generated design counts as "real" design work.
  5. Section on Sub-Topic 2: Automation and Technical Operations (multiple paragraphs)
    • AI-assisted cue programming
      Use scholarly sources + public posts to show how automation is changing the way technicians learn and work.
    • Reliability and safety concerns
      Use news + public posts to highlight worries about automated systems failing during live performances.
  6. Section on Sub-Topic 3: Workforce and Training (multiple paragraphs)
    • Impact on entry-level training
      Use scholarly sources + news to show concerns about new technicians missing hands-on learning opportunities.
    • Institutional pressure vs. worker experience
      Use public posts + news to show tensions between cost-saving goals and the need for skilled human labor.
  7. Conclusion
    Reflect on how the initial public conversation set the stage for the deeper issues explored in each sub-topic, and what questions remain for the field.
